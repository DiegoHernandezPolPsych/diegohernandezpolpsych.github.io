PhD candidate in political science

I study why some people refuse a partisan label, and what that refusal does to the way they reason about politics. My research brings the methods of political psychology to a group that most accounts of partisanship treat as a residual category: the pure independent.

My dissertation separates two routes into independence. Some people arrive through disaffection, a slow withdrawal from parties they have come to distrust. Others arrive through something closer to epistemic autonomy, a deliberate choice to weigh candidates and issues without a partisan shortcut. The two groups look identical on a standard party identification scale. They do not think alike. I trace the difference with pooled national survey data and a pair of survey experiments.

Research

Two ways of being independent

The dissertation argues that pure independence is not one thing. One pathway runs through disaffection, the other through deliberative autonomy, and the two carry different consequences for trust, engagement, and how people process political information.
